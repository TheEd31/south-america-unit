A History in Four Acts

Before & After Cortés

The Maya, the Aztec, and the Inca built civilizations that rivaled anything in the medieval world. This is the story of who they were — and what came after the ships arrived.

c. 1200 BC Present Day
↓
Prologue

A continent that was never empty.

Long before any European ship reached the Americas, the lands later called Latin America were home to some of the most sophisticated civilizations in human history. Cities of hundreds of thousands of people. Mathematical systems that included zero. Empires that spanned thousands of miles. Roads, calendars, hieroglyphic writing, hydraulic engineering, observatory astronomy.

The story that came later — that conquistadors discovered a "primitive" wilderness — was a lie told to justify what was done. The truth was always more interesting. And more devastating.

ACT ONE · c. 1200 BC – present

The Maya

Astronomers in the jungle.

Temple I at Tikal, Guatemala. Built around AD 700, the pyramid stretches over 150 feet above the jungle floor.

From roughly AD 250 to 900, the Maya built dozens of independent city-states across what is now southern Mexico, Guatemala, Belize, Honduras, and El Salvador. Each city-state was ruled by a god-king who served as the bridge between his people and the gods.

City-states, not an empire

Unlike the Aztec or Inca, the Maya never unified into one political state. Tikal, Palenque, Copán, Uxmal, and Chichén Itzá were all independent cities, sometimes allied, often at war. Tikal alone may have housed 100,000 people at its peak — making it one of the largest cities in the world in the year 700.

The "Three Sisters"

Maya agriculture rested on three crops grown together: maize, beans, and squash. Beans climbed the maize stalks; squash spread along the ground holding moisture in. Beyond simple slash-and-burn, the Maya engineered raised fields above swamps and cut terraces into hillsides — sophisticated techniques that supported enormous urban populations.

Mathematics and the sky

Maya mathematicians invented a concept of zero centuries before it appeared in Europe. Their base-20 number system allowed astronomical calculations of stunning precision.

Maya astronomers calculated the length of the solar year at 365.2420 days — off by just 0.0002 of a day from modern measurements. They predicted eclipses, tracked Venus, and built calendars accurate enough to schedule rituals 50 years in advance.

Two calendars ran simultaneously: a 260-day sacred calendar and a 365-day solar one. Every 52 years, the two realigned in a synchronization ceremony.

Players were not allowed to use their hands or feet, striking the ball with their hips, knees, and elbows. According to inscriptions, members of the losing team might be sacrificed.

On the Maya ball game, played in every major city

Writing, books, and what was lost

The Maya created the most sophisticated writing system in the ancient Americas — around 800 hieroglyphic symbols representing words and sounds. They recorded history on stone monuments (stelae) and in folding bark-paper books (codices).

In the 1500s, Spanish missionaries burned nearly every Maya book they found. Only three pre-conquest codices survived. What we know of Maya thought comes from inscriptions, the surviving codices, and the Popol Vuh, a creation story written down by Maya scholars after the conquest using the Latin alphabet.

The collapse

Sometime in the ninth century, the great Classic Maya cities of the southern lowlands were abandoned. Construction stopped. Populations crashed. The jungle returned.

Scholars now believe it was not one cause but several reinforcing each other: intensified warfare between city-states, overpopulation, deforestation and soil exhaustion, and finally a prolonged drought that the weakened system couldn't survive. Yet the Maya did not disappear. About seven million Maya people live today in Mexico and Central America, speaking dozens of related languages.

ACT TWO · 1325 – 1521

The Aztec

An empire of tribute, built on a lake.

The Pyramid of the Sun at Teotihuacan — a city that flourished 800 years before the Aztec arrived, but whose ruins they revered.

The Aztec did not invent the great culture of central Mexico. They inherited it — and reshaped it into something more centralized, more militarized, and more demanding than anything that came before.

From wanderers to founders

The people we call the Aztec called themselves the Mexica. They were latecomers — poor desert nomads who arrived in the Valley of Mexico around 1200, hired themselves out as mercenaries, and were despised by the established city-states.

According to legend, their god Huitzilopochtli told them to settle wherever they saw an eagle on a cactus holding a serpent. In 1325, they found that sign on a small island in Lake Texcoco. There they founded Tenochtitlan. (The image is still on the Mexican flag.)

The Triple Alliance

In 1428, Tenochtitlan joined with two neighboring cities, Texcoco and Tlacopan, to form the Triple Alliance. From this coalition the Aztec Empire grew.

Within a century, the empire covered roughly 80,000 square miles, ruled 38 provinces, and held 5–15 million subjects. Unlike Rome, it didn't try to assimilate its conquests — local rulers stayed in power as long as they paid tribute.

This was the empire's greatest strength and its fatal flaw. The tribute system generated enormous wealth — and equally enormous resentment.

Tenochtitlan: a city of canals

By 1500, Tenochtitlan housed somewhere between 200,000 and 400,000 people — larger than London, Paris, or Constantinople at the time. The city sat on an island connected to the mainland by three great causeways. Canals ran between districts. Aqueducts brought fresh water from springs in the hills. The central market at Tlatelolco drew 60,000 visitors a day.

At its heart stood the Templo Mayor, a twin pyramid dedicated to Huitzilopochtli (sun and war) and Tlaloc (rain). Spanish soldiers who first saw the city wondered, in their diaries, whether they were dreaming.

Why so many sacrifices

The Aztec believed Huitzilopochtli needed human blood to rise each morning. Without it, the sun would die and the world would end. This belief made human sacrifice a state necessity, not a religious option.

To supply victims, the Aztec waged flower wars — ritualized battles meant not to kill enemies but to capture them alive. Most sacrificial victims came from conquered or rival peoples, which meant Aztec military doctrine was structured around taking prisoners rather than killing them. This decision would prove catastrophic when it came up against Spanish soldiers who fought to kill.

When we saw all those cities and villages built in the water, and other great towns on dry land, we were astounded. Indeed, some of our soldiers asked whether it was not all a dream.

Bernal Díaz, eyewitness, on first seeing Tenochtitlan in 1519

ACT THREE · c. 1200 – 1572

The Inca

The largest empire ever built in the Americas.

Machu Picchu, built around 1450 — likely a royal estate of the emperor Pachacuti, never found by the Spanish.

From their capital at Cuzco, the Inca built an empire stretching 2,500 miles along the spine of the Andes — from Colombia to central Chile. At its height it contained 80 provinces, 16 million people, and dozens of languages, all governed from a single city above 11,000 feet.

Pachacuti, the World-Shaker

For two centuries the Inca were one small kingdom among many in the Andes. Then in 1438, the Chanca confederation attacked Cuzco. The reigning king fled. His son stood his ground, won the battle, and took the throne — and a new name: Pachacuti, meaning "World Transformer." Over the next thirty years he rebuilt Cuzco in stone and pushed the borders outward in every direction.

The Four Quarters

The empire was called Tawantinsuyu — "Land of the Four Quarters." Each quarter met at Cuzco's central plaza, the spiritual and administrative center of the world as the Inca understood it.

Government without writing

The Inca ran a continent-sized empire without an alphabet. They used quipus — knotted cords whose colors, positions, and branches encoded numerical data, census records, tribute schedules, and possibly narrative history.

Citizens owed labor through the mita system — set days of state service each year. In exchange, the state fed and clothed workers and stored food for famines. Some historians call this an early welfare state. Others call it forced labor. Both are partly right.

14,000 miles of road

The Qhapaq Ñan, the royal road, ran more than 14,000 miles through deserts, jungles, and mountain passes above 16,000 feet. It included paved highways, hanging bridges of woven grass over impossible gorges, tunnels through rock, and staircases climbing sheer cliffs.

Relay runners called chasquis could carry a message from one end of the empire to the other in about a week. The emperor in Cuzco ate fresh fish carried up from the Pacific coast in under two days.

Stone that outlasted conquest

Inca masons cut and fitted enormous stone blocks so precisely that a knife blade cannot slide between them. They did this without iron tools, mortar, or the wheel. Many original Inca walls in Cuzco are still standing today, holding up Spanish-era buildings constructed on top. The Spanish buildings have collapsed in earthquakes. The Inca foundations have not.

ACT FOUR · 1519 – 1572

The Conquest

Fewer than seven hundred men. Two empires.

It is tempting to credit the conquest to Spanish steel and gunpowder. The reality is more uncomfortable. Steel mattered. Horses mattered. But what mattered most was disease and division.

01

Cortés & the Aztec — 1519

Hernán Cortés landed on the Gulf Coast of Mexico with ~500 men, 16 horses, and a handful of cannons. He should have failed instantly. He didn't, because he immediately allied with the Tlaxcalans — a powerful nation that had resisted Aztec conquest for generations and welcomed any chance to strike back.

The decisive battles of the conquest were fought by Spanish-led Tlaxcalan armies numbering in the tens of thousands. Cortés provided the spark. Indigenous allies provided the fire.

02

The Fall of Tenochtitlan — 1521

After being expelled from the city during La Noche Triste (the Sad Night), Cortés returned with a massive Indigenous force and besieged Tenochtitlan. A smallpox epidemic — to which the Aztec had no immunity — swept through the trapped population, killing the new emperor Cuitláhuac and thousands of defenders.

After 75 days of siege, the last Aztec emperor Cuauhtémoc surrendered on August 13, 1521. The city that had astonished the world lay in ruins.

03

Pizarro & the Inca — 1532

Francisco Pizarro arrived in Peru with just 168 men and 62 horses. He should have been crushed in days. He wasn't, because smallpox had already arrived ahead of him, killing the emperor Huayna Capac and triggering a brutal civil war between his sons Huáscar and Atahualpa.

By the time Pizarro arrived, Atahualpa had just won the war. The empire was exhausted, divided, and full of conquered peoples who saw the Spanish as a chance to throw off Inca rule.

04

Cajamarca — November 1532

Pizarro invited Atahualpa to a meeting in the plaza of Cajamarca. The emperor arrived with thousands of unarmed retainers in ceremonial dress. Pizarro had hidden cavalry and cannons in the surrounding buildings.

The ambush lasted minutes. Thousands of Inca died. Atahualpa was captured. He paid a ransom of a room filled with gold and two more with silver — possibly the largest ransom in history — for his freedom. Pizarro accepted the ransom, then executed him anyway in August 1533.

Why so few men won

Spanish accounts emphasize gallant cavalry charges and divine providence. Modern scholarship tells a different story. Epidemic disease killed an estimated one-third to two-thirds of the Indigenous population within decades of contact — Indigenous immune systems had no defense against smallpox, measles, or influenza. Civil wars and old grievances split both empires before the Spanish arrived. Most of the soldiers fighting on the Spanish side were Indigenous people who saw conquest as liberation from another empire.

The conquest was real. The "few hundred Spaniards" was a myth.

ACT FIVE · 1521 – 1810

Three Centuries of Colonial Rule

A society built to extract wealth.

Spain organized its American empire as a pyramid — built to send silver, sugar, and gold back to Spain, and to keep everyone else in their assigned place.

Peninsulares
Born in Spain. Held the highest government and church positions. Tiny in number, vast in power.
Criollos
Of Spanish descent, born in the Americas. Often wealthy landowners — and resentful of being shut out of the top jobs.
Mestizos
Mixed European and Indigenous ancestry. A growing middle layer with limited rights.
Indigenous Peoples
The largest group in most colonies. Subjected to forced labor through the encomienda and mita systems.
Enslaved Africans
Trafficked in enormous numbers to work sugar plantations, mines, and ranches. At the bottom of the system by law.

The encomienda

Under the encomienda system, Spanish colonizers were granted the right to demand labor and tribute from Indigenous communities in exchange for protecting and Christianizing them. In practice, it produced conditions that killed people on a massive scale.

Potosí: the mountain that ate men

In 1545, the Spanish discovered one of the richest silver deposits in human history at Potosí, in what is now Bolivia. Over the colonial period, tens of millions of pesos worth of silver flowed from this single mountain to Spain. The cost in human life was almost incomprehensible: an estimated eight million Indigenous and enslaved African workers died in the Potosí mines.

The Church: education and erasure

The Catholic Church played a dual role in the colonies. Missionaries learned Indigenous languages, founded schools and hospitals, and recorded oral histories that would otherwise be lost. They also burned Indigenous books, destroyed temples, outlawed traditional ceremonies, and labeled Native religious traditions as devil-worship. The Maya codices burned at Mani in 1562 represent an irrecoverable loss to human knowledge.

And yet — Indigenous and African traditions survived in modified, hidden, blended forms. Festivals, foods, healing practices, and syncretic religions like Brazilian Candomblé and Cuban Santería carry these legacies into the present.

I have resolved to shake off the unbearable burden and the evil treatment we Indians receive from the Europeans, who have ruined our kingdoms and who behave with complete injustice toward our people.

Túpac Amaru II, at the start of the Great Andean Rebellion, 1780

In 1780, a Quechua nobleman named José Gabriel Condorcanqui took the name Túpac Amaru II — after the last Inca emperor — and led a massive uprising of Indigenous and mestizo people across Peru and Bolivia. At its height, the rebellion involved 100,000 followers. The Spanish crushed it brutally and executed Túpac Amaru II in a public spectacle designed to terrify others into submission. The rebellion failed. The memory of it has never died.

ACT SIX · 1791 – 1825

The Liberators

An empire shatters in thirty years.

Between 1791 and 1825, almost the entire Spanish empire in the Americas broke apart. The causes were Enlightenment ideas spreading through educated criollos, economic grievances over Spanish trade restrictions, and — most decisively — a crisis in Europe when Napoleon invaded Spain in 1808 and forced the king to abdicate.

HAITI · 1791–1804

Toussaint Louverture

Led enslaved Africans in Saint-Domingue against the armies of France, Spain, and Britain. The result: the only successful slave revolution in modern history, and the second independent nation in the Americas. The Haitian Revolution terrified slaveholders everywhere — and made future independence movements eager to not repeat it.

MEXICO · 1810

Miguel Hidalgo

A parish priest who, on September 16, 1810, rang the bell in the small town of Dolores and called for revolt against Spain. The Grito de Dolores (Cry of Dolores) is celebrated every year as Mexican Independence Day. Hidalgo was captured and executed in 1811, but the war he started would not end until 1821.

SOUTH AMERICA · 1819–1826

Simón Bolívar

Born in Caracas to a wealthy criollo family. Educated in Europe, soaked in Enlightenment philosophy. Between 1819 and 1826, "El Libertador" led campaigns that freed Venezuela, Colombia, Ecuador, Peru, and Bolivia from Spain. He dreamed of a unified federation of South American states. He died in 1830 watching that dream fall apart.

Do not adopt the best system of government, but the one that is most likely to succeed. Freedom, says Rousseau, is a succulent morsel, but one difficult to digest.

Simón Bolívar, Address to the Congress of Angostura, 1819

The aftermath: caudillos and disappointment

Independence didn't bring stability. The colonial period had concentrated political experience in tiny elites; when the Spanish left, those elites fought among themselves. Most new nations spent the nineteenth century cycling through caudillos — strongman military rulers who governed by force.

Bolívar's dream of a unified Gran Colombia collapsed within a decade. Near the end of his life he reportedly said: "I have plowed the sea."

EPILOGUE · 1825 – Present

What Survived

History, in Latin America, is not past tense.

The empires fell. The colonial system ended. The civilizations did not.

Today, the inheritance of this history is visible everywhere — in the languages people speak, the foods they cook, the music they make, the religions they practice, and the political fights still being waged across the region.

7M+

Maya Speakers

Across Mexico, Guatemala, and Belize. Yucatec, K'iche', Q'eqchi', and dozens more — all descendants of the Classic Period.

10M+

Quechua Speakers

The language Pachacuti made official is still spoken across the Andes. Bolivia and Peru both recognize it as a co-official language.

1.5M+

Nahuatl Speakers

The language of the Aztec is still spoken in central Mexico. Words like "chocolate," "tomato," "avocado," and "coyote" come from it.

2006

Indigenous Presidency

Bolivia elected Evo Morales, the country's first Indigenous head of state. Indigenous political movements continue to reshape the region.

14,000

Miles of Inca Road

The Qhapaq Ñan was declared a UNESCO World Heritage site in 2014, jointly nominated by six modern Andean nations.

~33M

Indigenous People in Latin America Today

Across hundreds of distinct nations and peoples. The descendants of the Maya, Aztec, Inca — and countless others — are still here, still speaking, still pushing for recognition.

The unfinished argument

Walk through the central plaza of almost any Latin American city and you'll find statues of conquistadors — some still standing, some toppled by protesters, some boarded up while their cities argue about what to do with them. The conquest is not a closed chapter. It is an active argument being had in real time about whose story counts as history, whose names belong on streets, and whose memory belongs in textbooks.

That argument is the legacy. So is the survival of the languages, the foods, the rhythms, the gods, and the people. The civilizations of the Americas were never truly conquered — only buried for a time. They have been coming back into view, on their own terms, ever since.
